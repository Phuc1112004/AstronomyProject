The Solar System (or Solar System) is a planetary system consisting of the Sun at the center and the objects around. The Solar System was formed from the collapse of a giant molecular cloud nearly 4.6 billion years ago. Most of the celestial bodies revolve around the Sun, and the mass is concentrated mainly on the eight planets whose orbits are nearly circular and the orbital plane closely coincides with each other called the ecliptic. The 4 inner planets include: Mercury, Venus, Earth and Mars - they are also called rocky planets because they are composed mainly of rocks and metals. The 4 outer gas giant planets are much more massive than the 4 inner planets. The two largest planets, Jupiter and Saturn, are composed mainly of helium and hydrogen; and the two outermost planets, Uranus and Neptune, are composed mainly of ice, such as water, ammonia and methane, and are sometimes classified as ice giants. There are 6 planets and 3 dwarf planets that have natural satellites orbiting them. These satellites are called "Moons" after the Earth's Moon. Each of the outer planets also has planetary rings of dust, particles, and small objects orbiting them.

The Moon orbits the Earth in the same direction as the Earth rotates and completes one orbital period when compared to fixed stars in about 27,322 days (a sidereal month) and one period when compared to the Earth's orbit. The Sun in about 29,530 days (a conjunction month). The Earth and the Moon orbit their celestial system's center of mass (common center of mass), located about 4,670 km (about 2,900 miles) from the center of the Earth (about 73% of the Earth's radius), forming a system. satellites are called the Earth–Moon system. On average, the distance to the Moon is about 385,000 km (239,000 miles) from the center of the Earth, which corresponds to about 60 Earth radii or 1,282 light seconds. With an average orbital velocity of 1,022 km/s, the Moon travels a distance approximately equal to its angular diameter, or about half a degree above the celestial sphere, per hour. The Moon differs from most satellites of the other planets in that its orbit is closer to the ecliptic rather than its principal plane (in this case, the Earth's equatorial plane). The plane of the Moon's orbit (the ecliptic) is inclined by about 5.1° to the ecliptic, while the equatorial plane of the Moon is tilted by only 1.5 degrees.

IS THE EARTH THE CENTER OF THE CELESTIAL SYSTEM?

The position of the Earth is only an insignificant part of the universe, despite recent theories that topple the foundations of modern cosmology, according to a University of British Columbia research team.
The book On the Revolutions of the Heavenly Spheres by the Polish astronomer Nicolaus Copernicus, in 1543, changed the concept from Earth as the center of the universe to Earth as just a planet orbiting the sun. Since then, generations of astronomers have expanded on this idea and formulated the Copenic Theory, which holds that our Earth in the universe is just a perfectly normal planet. Although the Copenic Theory has become the cornerstone of modern cosmology, finding evidence that the Earth is a non-specific part of the Universe has proved quite difficult.
In 1998, studies of cosmic explosions called "type Ia supernovae" showed that the expansion of the universe was accelerating, caused by the repulsion of a mysterious "dark energy" source. However, some scientists have put forward a different theory, stating that the Earth is located near the center of a giant "bubble" that is mostly devoid of matter, and goes against the Copenic Theory. Thereby, gravity would create the illusion of rapid expansion, similar to the effect of dark energy on supernova observations.
